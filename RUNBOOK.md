# Runbook

How this project is worked on. Not how it works — that is `DESIGN.md`, and not what it claims — that is `README.md`. This file is for whoever picks the work up cold, which so far has always been a language model with no memory of the last session.

It is deliberately not named `CLAUDE.md`. It is not meant to load itself into every conversation. Somebody has to point at it, on a day they have decided to work on this.

---

## Build

Debian 13. `g++ 14.2.0`, `GNU Make 4.4.1`. No dependencies, no build system beyond `core/Makefile`, no package to install. C++17, and `-Wall -Wextra -Wpedantic` is not decoration — the compiler has caught real defects here.

```
cd core
make test          # 128 assertions. Must print "ok (0 failures)".
make ratchet-run   # a measurement, not a test. Allowed to come out badly.
make pan           # play the pan (Era 0)
make dig           # play the dig (the geology field made playable)
make breaker       # play the breaker (crush the locked rock, pan out the ore)
make furnace       # play the furnace (roast the sulfide, smelt the ore to metal)
make carry         # play the carry (haul the locked heap from the hole to the stone)
make clean
```

Everything is header-only in `core/`. There are exactly seven translation units and each one is its own program.

`origin` is GitHub (`rossduprey/wrought`) and it is the single source of truth. Contributors fork it and open a pull request; maintainers push to `origin main`. (Maintainer environment specifics — the exact push path — live in an untracked local file, not in this repo.)

## What is in `core/`

Read them in this order. Each one only knows about the ones above it.

| file | owns |
|---|---|
| `phase_table.h` | Ten minerals, four Wentworth size bins. Densities, susceptibilities, aspect ratios. Every number `UNVERIFIED` or `AUTHORED`. |
| `substance.h` | `Substance`: a `[phase][size]` mass matrix, twice — free grains and composites. `grade()`. No position, no vertical dimension, no volume. |
| `settling.h` | Terminal velocity from a force balance, solved as a damped fixed point. Water properties from temperature; grain shape from the aspect ratio. `free_velocity(p, s, T)` is the number the whole project turns on, and the long comment on what a bin diameter *means* is the most load-bearing paragraph in `core/`. |
| `separate.h` | The Tromp partition, `9^σ` imperfection, screening, crushing, closed circuit, the bed (`exposed()`), `skin_depth()`, `recovery()`. |
| `magnetic.h` | The lodestone. A force balance on `magnetic_susceptibility` — `L = χ·B∇B/(μ₀ρg)`, grain volume cancels. Separates magnetite from everything on an axis orthogonal to the pan. Its two authored magnet numbers are asserted **not to matter**, because magnetite's χ/ρ is ~1375× the next phase's. |
| `smelt.h` | The bloomery. The first process that is chemistry, not mechanics — so it is a **tabulated reaction**, not a force balance. An element ledger (Fe/Si/O/C/other, from formulae and standard atomic weights) that balances across ore + charcoal → bloom + slag + gas. Its one finding is derived, not authored: with no flux, silica leaves as fayalite and eats **1.859 kg Fe per kg SiO₂**, so a charge smelts only above Fe/SiO₂ = 1.859. Also holds the **second and third metals**: `smelt_copper()` reduces cuprite (Cu₂O) on the same ledger, and the divergence is here — the furnace reads the *melting points* (moved here from `forge.h`) and pours copper **liquid** (`molten`) where iron stays a solid sponge, because a charcoal heat clears Cu's 1358 K but not Fe's 1811 K. Copper has no fayalite wall (silica does not eat it) and is diamagnetic (no lodestone gate). `smelt_tin()` reduces cassiterite (SnO₂) by the identical structure; tin's melting point (505 K) is so low that any reducing heat pours it — tin is *never* a sponge. `EL_SN` is on the ledger; both new pours share one `MeltResult`. And it holds the **sulfide gate**: `roast()` handles chalcocite (Cu₂S, `CHALCOCITE`), which a reducing fire *cannot* smelt (it strips oxygen; a sulfide has none) — an oxidizing roast (opposite atmosphere, gated on `ROAST_T`) burns the sulfur off as SO₂ and leaves cuprite the furnace can then reduce. Copper conserved, sulfur → gas, the new oxide's oxygen atmospheric (like the bloomery blast). Two fires, opposite atmosphere, fixed order — sulfur's second verdict, cured at the furnace where iron's rode to the anvil. |
| `forge.h` | The forge, both halves. **Consolidation** (bloom → bar): the first deformation process — squeezes a porous solid instead of sorting grains. A bloom is a slag-filled sponge; slag drains only through connected pores, so below a percolation floor the rest is trapped as stringers (~1.5%, connectivity not effort). Every heat also scales iron away, so a cleaner bar is a smaller one — the grade/recovery law a fourth time. **Shaping** (bar → tool): gives a form and work-hardens it, coupled through temperature — *form is free at any heat, hardness is only cold* (above the recrystallization floor, ~0.4 Tm, hot work leaves no hardness). And shaping holds the chain's last finding, **red-short**: a bar carrying dissolved sulfur (seeded by pyrite in the bloomery, `smelt.h`) cracks when worked *hot*, because above the Fe–FeS eutectic (~988 °C, verified) a liquid film wets its grain boundaries — so dirty iron red-shorts at the hot end where clean iron flows free, and is merely sound (never workable) at the cold end. Floors derived (geometry, melting-point fractions, the eutectic), the drain/scale/hardening/partition/cracking *rates* authored (#22, #23, #24). Also holds the **copper fork**: `cast()` turns a liquid copper melt into a **clean** billet (slag floats off a melt, so it skips consolidation and the connectivity floor), and `stock()`/`draw()`/`hardness()` are now metal-neutral — a cast billet and an iron bar become the same `Tool`, hardened by the same law on each metal's own recryst floor (a fraction of its own melting point, carried on `Tool.tm`). Iron and copper diverge at the furnace, rejoin at the hammer. And the **alloy fork**: `alloy()` mixes a copper pour and a tin pour into a bronze `Billet`; the tin fraction sets two numbers the existing law reads — it *depresses* the melting point (`bronze_tm`) and *lifts* the whole hardness curve (`bronze_h_annealed`/`bronze_h_saturated`, carried on `Tool.h_annealed`/`h_saturated`). Same `cast`/`stock`/`draw`/`hardness`; alloying moves inputs, never the law. So cast bronze is harder than cast copper with no cold work, and pours cooler than copper — one solute, both gifts. |
| `levigate.h` | The batch decant (partition is a ramp, exact, no authored sharpness) and **flocculation** (#15): the deflocculated state is the bare-platelet model everything else assumes; `flocculate()` kills the clay's charge so it drops as a floc of an authored size (#29), which recovers the clay in minutes and divides it from clay-sized quartz by surface charge — a variable velocity cannot read. |
| `fire.h` | The two bridges from a body of clay to a tool: grit → sharpness, clay → vessel. The first is **derived** now — the log law over a rough bed — and keeps one bounded shape factor where it used to keep an invented functional form. The second is still invented end to end. Both are issues. Read the "two misplacements" note at the foot of its header before you touch `fire_pan`. |
| `geology.h` | The ground, and the project's **first spatial model** — composition as a deterministic field `f(x, y, tier)`. `sample(place, tier, mass)` is the scoop the whole chain feeds on; it returns a `Substance`, so nothing downstream had to change. Most of the valley is barren quartz; an ore body grades smoothly to rich at a center (no "nodes"). Depth is a discrete `DepthTier` (SURFACE/MIDDLE/DEEP), not a carved z — and it changes the *mineral*: a copper body reads `CUPRITE` (oxide cap) up top and `CHALCOCITE` (sulfide root, must be roasted) at depth. `dig_column()` sums the tiers into one **mixed pile**. What comes up is a **size distribution**, not one locked grade (corrected 2026-07-11): `sample()` spreads the won rock over a Gates-Gaudin-Schuhmann PSD and splits each size against the mineral's **liberation size** (`liberation_bin()` — cassiterite frees at gravel, cuprite at sand, chalcocite only at clay). So the **fine** end comes up free (pannable at the hole, no breaker) and the **coarse** end locked (`crush()` it — the verb already existed). Placer tin comes up ~all free; disseminated sulfide ~all locked; the oxide cap between — sourced grain sizes, not knobs. Peak grade is capped at `COMPOSITE_TARGET_FRACTION` (0.5) since a locked grain is half mineral. The two `DEPOSITS` (copper hill, tin creek) sit hundreds of meters apart so no spot yields both — **co-location is the gate**, now geometry not prose. The whole valley layout, `N_TIER`, and the linear falloff are authored placeholders (#28). Tests assert only the field's *shape* (barren-by-default, monotone grading, depth-changes-mineral, co-location, and placer-frees-at-the-face-while-sulfide-waits-for-the-breaker). |

`test_separation.cpp` is the suite. `pan.cpp`, `dig.cpp`, `breaker.cpp`, `furnace.cpp` and `carry.cpp` are the five playable slices — `pan.cpp` plays the Era-0 wash; `dig.cpp` plays the geology field (`make dig`): a vertical dig-bar you strike down through the depth tiers with hand or pick, the assay changing *mineral* (oxide cap → sulfide root) as you descend, and coarse locked rock you shoulder off toward the breaker; `carry.cpp` plays the haul (`make carry`): the hole-to-stone loop with two gestures — **wash** the free fines where you stand, and **haul** the locked coarse with rungs from hands to a cart you build — teaching that at this hard-rock hill the wash wins only a sliver and the copper is locked in the coarse you must carry (it would be the reverse at the placer tin ground); `breaker.cpp` plays what the dig hands you (`make breaker`): you strike the locked rock with a hammer or sledge — a liberation bar climbing over a grind profile going to dust — then pan it, and the grade/recovery law lands as the choice of *when to stop hammering* (crush too little, the pan wins nothing; too much, the ore washes over the lip); `furnace.cpp` plays the fire (`make furnace`): the draft decides whether it reduces or roasts, and the sulfide must be roasted before the reducing fire can pour it. `ratchet.cpp` is none of them — it is an instrument, it prints tables, and it exists because `DESIGN.md` asserted something without ever having run it.

## The rules

**An uncited number is a bug.** This is `CONTRIBUTING.md`'s rule and it governs the code as much as the design. A number that came from a language model's memory ships as `UNVERIFIED` and gets a tracked issue. A number that was chosen by a human because it felt right ships as `AUTHORED` and gets a tracked issue. The distinction matters: an unverified number has a correct value somebody could look up, and an authored one does not.

**Distinguish authored from derived, out loud, in the comment.** The single most valuable habit here. `settling.h`'s clay lower bound of 0.2 µm is authored, and the file says so; the three-hundred-fold settling gap that falls out of it is derived, and nobody chose it. When a finding rests on a bridge, name the bridge.

**A measurement is allowed to come out badly.** `make ratchet-run` was written to confirm the design document's central mechanism and it refuted it instead. That is the best day this project has had. Do not fix an instrument that is telling you something you did not want to hear.

**When a claim turns out to be wrong, the wrong claim stays.** Written next to the right one, dated, with what caused the error. There are two preserved in `levigate.h`, one in `README.md`'s claim #2, one on `README.md`'s front page, and one each in `fire.h`, `ratchet.cpp`, `test_separation.cpp` and `DESIGN.md`. They are not self-flagellation; they are the only record of *why* the current sentence is worded the way it is, and without them somebody will helpfully revert it. Two of them are wrong claims about wrong claims.

**Grade and recovery are always reported together.** One of them alone is a lie by omission. Reporting either as a single score invites optimising it, and the composition vector — not a score — is the state.

**Teach the tool, never the wisdom.** Any interface may explain how an input maps to an action. No interface may state the strategy that emerges from using it. If the player can be told the answer, the simulation was decoration.

## How the work actually goes

1. Probe in the scratchpad. Throwaway `.cpp` files that link the headers and print a table. Do not put a probe in the repo; do steal its findings.
2. If it found something, decide whether it is a *test* (an invariant that must hold) or a *measurement* (a number we want to watch). Tests go in `test_separation.cpp`. Measurements get their own program.
3. Let the result contradict the design document. Then change the design document, and say in the commit that it lost.
4. New authored numbers get an issue before the commit that introduces them lands, tagged `authored-number` or `unverified-number`.
5. Commit, push to `origin`.

Commit messages are lowercase, `area: what changed`, and they are allowed to be about what was learned rather than what was typed — `core: levigation, and the ratchet does not turn`.

## Where we are

Era 0 (hands, pan, sluice, lodestone) and Era 1 (levigation, fired pot) are implemented and measured, and Era 2 is underway: the bloomery smelts and iron falls out of it (`smelt.h`), and the forge both consolidates that bloom into a bar and shapes the bar into a hardened tool (`forge.h`, both halves 2026-07-10). The iron chain now runs dig → pan → crush → lodestone → bloomery → forge (consolidate → shape), end to end — the first full process chain in the project, and it now has a *failure mode*: **red-short** (added 2026-07-10) closes the loop by making a sulfur-bearing bar crack when it is worked hot. Sulfur is on the element ledger (`EL_S`), pyrite seeds it into the metal in the bloomery, and it rides the metal to the anvil where hot work above the Fe–FeS eutectic tears the grain boundaries. And now there is a **second metal**: **copper** (added 2026-07-10) shares the front of the chain and *diverges at the furnace* — `smelt_copper()` reduces cuprite on the same ledger and pours it **liquid** (cast, and clean) where iron stays a solid sponge, because the same charcoal heat clears copper's melting point but not iron's. The two paths *rejoin at the anvil*: a cast billet is drawn and hardened by the same metal-neutral `draw()`/`hardness()`, on copper's own lower recryst floor. `EL_CU` is on the ledger; `CUPRITE` and `COPPER` are on the phase table. And now there is a **third metal and the first alloy**: **tin and bronze** (added 2026-07-10). `smelt_tin()` reduces cassiterite by the same structure — tin melts so low (505 K) it is never a sponge, so its historical gate was geology, not metallurgy — and `alloy()` mixes a copper pour with a tin pour into **bronze**, which lands off the end of both parents: harder than either (solid-solution strengthening lifts the hardness curve) *and* lower-melting than copper (tin depresses the freeze). One solute, both gifts; cast, stocked, drawn and hardened by the very same functions. `EL_SN`, `CASSITERITE`, `TIN` are on the ledger and phase table. And copper's furnace story now closes on **sulfide ore**: **roasting** (added 2026-07-10). The oxide ores are the weathered surface; below them copper is a sulfide (`CHALCOCITE`, Cu₂S) the reducing fire cannot smelt, because a charcoal fire strips *oxygen* and a sulfide has none. `roast()` runs an *oxidizing* fire first — the opposite atmosphere — burning the sulfur off as SO₂ and leaving a copper oxide the furnace then reduces normally. Two fires of opposite atmosphere in a fixed order, and it is sulfur's second verdict: invisible-and-incurable in iron (red-short, caught at the anvil), loud-and-curable in copper (caught at the furnace). The project's separation-and-deformation physics now spans three metals, one alloy, and both faces of the sulfur problem. And under all of it there is now **ground**: the project's first *spatial* model (`geology.h`, 2026-07-10). Ore is no longer conjured for a test — it comes from a field `f(x, y, tier)` you sample by pointing at the dirt. Depth is a tier that changes the *mineral* (oxide cap over sulfide root, so the roast lives underground), a full-depth dig heaps a **mixed column** — and it comes up as *locked rock* that must be hauled (hands, then a cart) to a **rock-breaking station** and crushed (`separate.h crush`) before it can be cobbed, so nothing is pre-liberated and the breaker is a place you walk to. Copper and tin are placed far apart so **co-location is the gate** is finally geometry: bronze forces you to travel.

**This section said, on 2026-07-09, that #13 was "the single most dangerous uncited number in the project — it can reverse a finding rather than refine one." On 2026-07-10 it was done, and it did.** One of the four settled results below is now struck through. Read that as encouragement: the paragraph was right, the work was worth prioritising, and the way to find out was to go and do the arithmetic. **#10 was done the same day, for the same reason, and it reversed a different thing than anyone expected.** Of the three results that remain, one is now derived rather than provisional:

- **Progression is a staircase, not a ratchet.** You do not refine a tool into a better tool; you invent a different mechanism with its own irreducible sharpness. *This used to say "overturned if the grit → sharpness bridge in `fire.h` has the wrong functional form — its magnitude is safe by three orders of magnitude, its exponent is not."* **The bridge did have the wrong functional form, and the staircase gained margin rather than losing it.** #10 replaced it with the log law over a rough bed: the exponent is `1 + 1/ln(15·d_cut/d_grit)` ≈ 1.11, *super*-linear rather than the feared `√(d/h)`, and roughness cannot blur the pan past 3.4% at any grit size whatever. σ is 0.5500 at every generation to nine places. **Within this model nothing is left that could overturn it:** a pan's sharpness has exactly two inputs — the operator's wrist (#5, which sets a step's height, not whether there are steps) and the floor's roughness (derived). Restoring the spiral now requires a *new mechanism* by which finer clay makes a sharper pan, and none is proposed. *(It also survived #13 untouched, which was the obvious thing to fear and did not happen — a clay platelet has no opinion about sand.)*
- **Levigation authors nothing.** Its imperfection is exactly 3.0 for every vessel, charge and wait, and even the pour fraction is derived. Nothing to overturn — it is algebra. *(Survived #13: the algebra never moved. Every number it stood on did.)*
- ~~**Era 1 has a lodestone-shaped hole.** Levigation cannot separate clay from clay-sized quartz; the velocity ratio is 1.031×, the same 1.03× that hides magnetite in hematite.~~ **Overturned 2026-07-10 by #13.** The 1.031× was the ratio between two *spheres* of equal diameter and unequal density; kaolinite is a platelet and the real ratio is **6.977×**. Levigation divides the clay bin, reaches grade 1.000 given patience, and has a grade/recovery curve like every other separator here. Deflocculation (**#15**) survives as an *improvement*, not a *requirement*.
- **Throughput is a bigger shovel.** More mass per hour is not progression. A bigger pot is, because it moves the curve outward at matched grade. This distinction was got wrong once already, in the direction of thinking the pot's loop was illegal. *(Survived #13, but its old proof did not: "same grade, more recovery" was only sayable while grade was pinned. The pot now wins at every matched grade, which is the real test and the one §2a specifies.)*
- **Iron has a wall, and it is 1.859.** *(Added 2026-07-10 by `smelt.h`.)* A fluxless bloomery loses 1.859 kg of iron to fayalite slag per kg of silica — two atomic weights, nobody's choice — so a charge smelts only above Fe/SiO₂ = 1.859. Panned sand assays 0.12 and washes no higher than ≈ 0.5, so **the pan cannot make smeltable ore.** This is the concrete form of "possibility is not the gate, efficiency is," and it is why the lodestone is not romantic decoration: crush-and-magnet is the only Era-1 path across the wall. If any of `phase_table.h`'s densities or `substance.h`'s `COMPOSITE_TARGET_FRACTION` move, the exact ratios shift but the wall does not — it is stoichiometry, not fixture.
- **A wrought bar is never clean, and the floor is connectivity.** *(Added 2026-07-10 by `forge.h`.)* Hammering drains slag only through pores that still connect; below a percolation threshold the rest is trapped as stringers. Blooms that start with 3× different slag converge on the *same* residual (~1.5%), and hammering past saturation moves it by a part in a billion — so the floor is set by geometry, not effort, and it is why wrought iron is wrought. And driving the bar cleaner scales iron away: the grade/recovery law, found a fourth time. The findings ride the *signs* of the authored rates (#22), not their values; move `CONNECTIVITY_FLOOR` or `RHO_SLAG` and the 1.5% shifts but the floor's existence and effort-independence do not.
- **Form is free at any heat; hardness is only cold.** *(Added 2026-07-10 by `forge.h` shaping.)* A draw gives the same elongation whether the metal is hot or cold — form is volume conservation, and volume does not care about temperature. Hardness is different: it comes from dislocations, and above the recrystallization floor (~0.4 of iron's melting point, ~450 °C) the metal re-grows fresh grain as fast as the hammer tangles it, so hot work leaves *no* hardness behind. Only cold strain accumulates, monotone and saturating. That is the smith's bind, and it is physics not skill: metal moves easily hot but a hard edge can only be beaten in cold — form and hardness are won at opposite ends of the heat. The recryst floor is a melting-point fraction (derived), the hardening rate/ceiling authored (#23); the finding rides the floor's existence and hardening's shape, not the numbers.
- **The contaminant no separator can see is the one that breaks the tool.** *(Added 2026-07-10 by `forge.h` red-short.)* Sulfur is not magnetic, does not settle apart, and the bloomery's fluxless acidic slag is a poor sink for it — so pyrite's sulfur survives the pan, the sluice, the lodestone *and* the furnace, dissolving into the reduced iron and riding it, invisible, to the anvil. There it inverts the shaping finding: above the Fe–FeS eutectic (~988 °C, verified — far below a forging heat) a liquid film wets the grain boundaries, so a sulfur-bearing bar **red-shorts** — it cracks apart under *hot* work, exactly where clean iron flows free. Cold, the film is solid and the bar holds, but cold it will not move and it hardens toward brittleness. So dirty iron is trapped at both ends of the heat, which is why pyritic ore was worthless despite being iron-rich — and why the lodestone and the crush were never only about yield. The eutectic is verified; the sulfur *threshold*, the metal/slag *partition*, and the *cracking rate* are authored (#24) — the finding rides the eutectic sitting below forging heat and cracking being monotone in hot sulfur-strain, not the values.
- **Abundance is not the gate; melting point is.** *(Added 2026-07-10 by `smelt.h`/`forge.h` copper.)* The second metal shares the whole front of the chain and parts from iron at the furnace, and the thing that parts them is two melting points against one fire: a charcoal hearth (~1150–1300 °C) clears copper's 1085 °C but falls short of iron's 1538 °C, so the identical fire pours copper **liquid** (cast) and leaves iron a **solid** sponge (forged). Nobody chose that — the melting points did — and it is why copper metallurgy is millennia older than iron's despite iron being far commoner. State also decides *cleanliness*: because it pours liquid, copper sheds its slag by density and casts clean, escaping the connectivity floor that leaves wrought iron ~1.5% stringers — that floor was never about iron, it was about being solid at furnace heat. Then the two rejoin at shaping: `draw()`/`hardness()` are now metal-neutral and read each metal's own recryst floor (a fraction of its own melting point), so a heat that cold-works iron can anneal copper — one law, two floors. The melting points are verified; copper's reduction-onset temperature and its reuse of iron's hardness *scale* are authored (#25) — the finding rides the furnace heat sitting between the two melting points, not the rates.
- **Alloying is not averaging.** *(Added 2026-07-10 by `smelt.h`/`forge.h` tin + bronze.)* Tin is the easiest metal in the chain to smelt — cassiterite reduces like copper's ore and tin melts below a cooking fire, so it is *never* a sponge — yet it was the scarce, traded half of the age, because its binding constraint is not any process in the code but *where the ore is*: **smeltability is not the gate, co-location is**, and bronze needing both a common metal and a rare one is why it forced long-distance trade. And the alloy itself lands *off the end* of both parents rather than between them: a tenth of tin makes copper harder than either soft parent *and* lower-melting than copper. One cause does both — a tin atom is the wrong size for a copper lattice site, and that foreignness strains the solid (snagging dislocations, so cast bronze takes an edge with no cold work) *and* disorders the freeze (so it pours cooler). It is cast, stocked, drawn and hardened by the *same* functions as pure copper, entering the anvil with a lifted hardness curve and a depressed melting point — so cold-worked bronze tops cold-worked copper and even cold-worked wrought iron, which is why the coming of iron did not at once retire bronze. The melting points are verified; the depression rate and the solid-solution hardening rate are authored (#26) — the finding rides their *signs* (tin lowers the melt, raises the hardness, monotonically), not their magnitudes.
- **One element, two verdicts: sulfur is cured at the furnace or hidden to the anvil.** *(Added 2026-07-10 by `smelt.h` roasting.)* Copper's deep ore is a sulfide, and a reducing fire has no move on it — it strips oxygen, and a sulfide has none to give, so chalcocite fed straight to `smelt_copper` yields *zero* metal and its sulfur reports to slag. Winning it takes a second fire that runs the *other way*: an oxidizing **roast** that adds oxygen (from air) and drives the sulfur off as SO₂, leaving a copper oxide the furnace then reduces to the *same clean cast* an oxide ore would give — two fires of opposite atmosphere, in a fixed order, neither sufficient alone. This is the exact inverse of red-short: the *same* villain, sulfur, is loud at copper's furnace (the sulfide flatly refuses to smelt) and curable right there, where in iron it is silent at the furnace and rides invisibly to the anvil, incurable. The difference is not the sulfur; it is that copper's sulfide roasts cleanly to a reducible oxide and iron's contaminant does not. The reaction and its atmospheric oxygen are tabulated; the roast-onset temperature and the one-roast-takes-all-sulfur idealization are authored (#27) — the finding rides the sulfide having no oxygen for a reducing fire to take, not any rate.
- **Deflocculation's honest half is flocculation, and it reads a variable velocity cannot.** *(Added 2026-07-10 by #15.)* The model already assumed the deflocculated (bare-platelet) state, and a probe killed the tempting derived model for the rest: single-particle Brownian suspension does not hold clay up at pottery scale (Péclet ~10⁴), so "deflocculated clay stays up forever" is a many-body colloidal effect needing the six invented numbers #15 warned against. What is clean is `flocculate()`: kill the charge and the clay drops as a floc of an authored size (#29) in ~73 s versus a platelet's ~158 h — recovery on demand — and since clay-sized quartz keeps its charge and will not floc, the recovered solid is *purer than the liquor* (0.999 from a 0.862 feed), dividing a single velocity class by surface charge. #13 split that class by shape; this splits it by an orthogonal variable, the lodestone's Era-0 role carried into Era 1. Both findings ride on the floc being far coarser than a platelet, not on its size. This was the last unbuilt mechanism; the core is done.
- **A stony floor swallows the feed; it does not blur it — and now the arithmetic charges it.** *(Added 2026-07-10 by #19.)* A grain finer than a pot's floor grit wedges below the height the flow reaches and does not move at any cut, so it stays in the pan whatever its velocity — a *second misplacement*, the same lesson `screen()` learned about size on 2026-07-09 and `fire.h` split out of the blur on 2026-07-10, now **applied** in `separate()`. `fire_pan` fills a per-bin `shelter[]` from the geometry already derived for #10 (the grain's centre below `z₀` — a hiding–exposure function, so no new authored number), and the sheltered share reports to the concentrate unsorted. The effect is the one the panel always showed and could not charge: a raw-dirt pot's *measured* enrichment collapses to 1.21×, **below cupped hands (1.26×)**, while a levigated pot reaches 1.59× — the whole ratchet rung is the shelter column going to zero, not σ. The empirical hiding–exposure fits (Egiazaroff 1965, Wilcock & Crowe 2003) the issue named would have imported constants; the derived geometry did not need them.
- **Co-location is the gate, and now it is geometry.** *(Added 2026-07-10 by `geology.h`.)* The ground became a deterministic field `f(x, y, tier)`: you sample by pointing at the dirt, most of the valley is barren, and an ore body grades smoothly to a center — no nodes. Two things the rest of the chain was already built to pay off fall out of it. **Depth changes the mineral**, not just the amount: a copper body is oxide (cuprite) at the surface and sulfide (chalcocite) at depth, so "the gossans were worked before the deep sulfides" is now literally how far down you dug, and a full-depth dig heaps a **mixed column** of oxide + sulfide + waste. It comes up as **locked rock** (the dig fills the `composite` bins), so a scoop reads a grade but liberates nothing until it is hauled to a **rock-breaking station** and `crush()`ed to free the grains, then **panned** to part ore from waste by density — the honest chain is *dig locked rock → haul (hands → a cart you build) → crush → pan → furnace*. **This paragraph said, until 2026-07-11, that the freed rock is `cobbed` — "the Era-0 verb finally has a job."** A probe building `breaker.cpp` refuted it: cobbing (`HAND_COB`) is a *size* screen, the dig delivers ore and gangue at the *same* size (both `GRAVEL`), so cobbing enriches nothing on this feed — and after crushing it keeps the coarse remainder while the freed ore falls fine, so it actively throws the ore away. The verb that parts freed ore from freed gangue is the **pan** (density). History agrees and names the order — the Cornish floors ran spalling → cobbing → *bucking* (crush) → *jigging* (gravity): cobbing was a *pre*-crush hand-sort **by eye**, which we do not model as a player verb (it wants a sort-the-pile client interaction outside the playability window). See `breaker.cpp`'s head. And **copper and tin are placed far apart**, so no hole yields both: the tin finding's unbuildable half (tin is *rare*, which forced Bronze-Age trade) becomes distance you cross — and the cart is what makes crossing it worth the haul. The engine choice that made it free: depth is a tier the dig bar crosses, not carved terrain — the hole is cosmetics, the sim only ever sees the `Substance` that comes up, so the ground stays a cheap flat plane. Everything authored (valley layout, `N_TIER`, linear falloff, peak grades now capped at 0.5) is tracked in #28; the tests assert only the field's shape.

Three habits earned their keep on 2026-07-10 and are worth stating as rules rather than as anecdotes.

**An uncited number can be load-bearing for a conclusion in a different file, and nothing in the code will tell you which one** — the velocity ratio that Era 1 rested on was a property of the word "sphere", which nobody had ever written down as an assumption.

**A correction can be wrong.** `levigate.h` carried a dated, self-flagellating note admitting its algebra had been too clever about vessel depth; the algebra was right, and the note was covering for a defect one file over. Both stay, both dated. Do not assume the most recent sentence is the true one.

**A lesson learned in one file does not propagate to the next one by itself.** `separate.h` worked out on 2026-07-09 that *a separator has two misplacements, not one*, and rewrote `screen()` around it. `fire.h` was making the identical mistake about pot floors — one authored line, one function away, charging the cost of a grain that never moved to the blur of the grains that did — and it survived a full day and a passing test suite. When you fix a modelling error, grep for the shape of it, not for the symbol.

**A step's dependencies are not always the ones the plan drew.** This very section said, right here, that smelt "is downstream of nothing." It is downstream of *two* things — the lodestone and the crush — and the way that surfaced was a probe that tried to feed a bloomery from a pan and got a glassy slag. The plan's dependency graph is a hypothesis like any other; the arithmetic is what actually knows the edges. Build the probe before you believe the ordering.

**Copper, tin, bronze and sulfide roasting are now built** (2026-07-10) — the chain generalizes across three metals, its first alloy, and both faces of the sulfur problem: the same ledger, the same anvil, a different reduction that changes the metal's *state* at the furnace, an alloying step that moves the numbers the shaping law reads without touching the law, and a roast that adds oxygen precisely so a later fire can take it away. **Sulfide copper is done** — `roast()` desulfurizes chalcocite to a smeltable oxide, the copper cousin of red-short caught at the furnace instead of the anvil. **And the geological half of the tin finding is now done too** (`geology.h`): ore scarcity is a field, and "co-location is the gate" is geometry, not prose. **The dig now hands you locked rock, not clean grains** (closed 2026-07-10): `sample()` fills the `composite` bins, so a scoop reads a *grade* on the panel but liberates nothing a separator can win until it is hauled to the **rock-breaking station** and `crush()`ed — the verb already existed, the dig just had to feed the locked side. Peak grade is capped at `COMPOSITE_TARGET_FRACTION` (0.5) because a locked grain is half mineral. That closes the pre-liberated shortcut the last build surfaced. What it *surfaced in turn*, not started, is the **game layer**: the player-built **cart** and the carry-capacity rungs (hands → back-load → cart) that make hauling heavy waste rock to the breaker a real cost, plus the assay-panel and dig-bar UI — the sim core now produces exactly what they will consume. **And the sheltered grain now has somewhere to go** (#19, closed 2026-07-10): `separate()` carries a per-bin `shelter[]` second misplacement, `fire_pan` fills it, and a stony pot's swallowed fines report to the concentrate unsorted — so its measured enrichment falls below cupped hands, and the ratchet instrument reads it through an actual separation instead of off σ. **And deflocculation is now built too** (#15, closed 2026-07-10), which was the last unbuilt *mechanism* in the core. A probe settled how much of it is honest: single-particle Brownian suspension does *not* hold clay up at pottery scale (Péclet ~10⁴), so the "stays up forever" half is a many-body colloidal effect we refuse (the six invented numbers #15 itself warned against); what we model is the clean half — `flocculate()` drops the clay as a floc of an authored size (#29) in minutes instead of the ~158 h a platelet needs, and because quartz will not floc the recovered solid is purer than the liquor (0.999 from 0.862), a separation by surface charge orthogonal to velocity. **With #15 built the core has no remaining unbuilt mechanism** — every other open issue is a citation chore (**#1, #2, #5, #7, #12, #16, #17, #18, #20, #21, #22, #23, #24, #25, #26, #27, #29**), so the core is at the finish line the "stay in the core until it's done" pass aimed at, and the UI work can begin on a finished product. This session's remaining **#28** debt is only the authored layout: the valley layout (deposit centers, radii, peak grades), `N_TIER`, and the linear grade falloff are placeholders — none of which can reverse the finding they sit under (*co-location is the gate*), which rides only on the field's *shape* (barren-by-default, monotone grading, depth-changes-mineral, copper-and-tin-never-together, dug-ore-locked-until-crushed), asserted directly and independent of any layout number.

**And the game layer has begun** (2026-07-11): `dig.cpp` is the second playable slice, the geology field made playable the way `pan.cpp` made the wash playable. The input gesture — decided with Ross across earlier sessions and, until now, written down nowhere — is finally recorded (here, in `DESIGN.md`'s geology section, and in the file's own head): a **vertical bar of the layers you are cutting**, struck down through the depth tiers with **hand or shovel**, one strike one bite, the pit edge descending in real time — the honest terminal form of a left-click eating into the dirt. What it teaches, and never states: the mineral *changes* with depth (a shallow hole is a small clean oxide pile, a deep one a big mixed oxide+sulfide pile that costs a sort and a second fire), and co-location is footsteps (copper here, tin a long walk east). It ends where the honest chain says it must — you shoulder **locked rock** and leave for the breaker, carrying a grade you cannot yet keep.

**And the breaker is now built too** (`breaker.cpp`, 2026-07-11): the third playable slice, and the one that catches the rock the dig hands off. You strike the locked heap with a **hammer or sledge** — one strike one blow, `separate.h crush()` under it — and the persistent display is the rock itself: a **liberation bar** (how much ore is freed) over a **grind profile** (how coarse the pile still is, gravel down to dust), both things an eye can read off a real heap and *neither* a projected pan result, because showing "recovery is falling now" would state the very wisdom the slice is here to teach. When you judge it broken enough you **pan** it, and only then learn the cost of the choice: crush too little and the ore is still locked and the pan wins nothing; crush too much and it is dust that washes over the lip. Grade climbs the whole way, recovery peaks (~3 heavy blows) and falls — the project's grade/recovery law a seventh time, now as a gesture. Building it **retired a claim**: the chain was *not* crush→cob (see the geology finding above and `breaker.cpp`'s head); cobbing is a size sort useless on the dig's same-size feed, and the real second verb is the pan. Still unbuilt after it: the **carry loop** (cart, carry rungs hands→back-load→cart) that makes hauling the heavy waste rock from dig to breaker a real cost, and the **furnace/roast station** the concentrate then feeds.

**And the furnace is now built** (`furnace.cpp`, 2026-07-11): the fourth playable slice, and the one that finally turns ore into **metal**. It takes the breaker's concentrate — still rock, mixing red **oxide** (cuprite) and dark **sulfide** (chalcocite) copper in one heap — and runs on exactly `smelt.h`'s verbs, `roast()` and `smelt_copper()`. The whole decision is the **draft**: bank it under charcoal and the fire *reduces* (strips oxygen, pours the oxide's metal); open it to the air and it *oxidizes* (a roast, burning the sulfur off as SO₂). A reducing fire cannot touch the sulfide — it has no oxygen to strip — so you must **roast it first** to put oxygen on before the fire can take it off: two fires of opposite atmosphere, in the one order that works. The persistent display is the **fire's colour** (a smith reads heat as dull-red→cherry→orange→yellow→white; the 1073 K roast/reduce onset lands at orange and the 1357 K clean pour at yellow-white, so the colour *is* the honest gauge) over the **charge as its materials** (red oxide, dark sulfide, stony gangue — whose proportions you watch shift as a roast eats the sulfide), and *nowhere* a projected yield. Verified honest across three outcomes: raw smelt pours **45%** and sends the sulfide to slag; roast-then-smelt pours **63%**; a fire below orange refuses without spending the charge.

Building it surfaced a finding the docs did not have, and it is the best part: **`roast()` only cures the sulfide the *crushing* freed.** A chalcocite grain still sealed in gangue — rock the hammer left whole at the breaker — roasts incompletely and no fire reaches it (`smelt.h roast()`, "honest"). So the recovery ceiling here (63%, not 100%) was **set upstream, at the breaking stone**; the furnace is where you find out the breaker mattered. The slice reports that loss *split* from the sulfide you simply never roasted, because they are different mistakes: one is a fire you did not run, the other is a blow you did not strike.

**And the carry loop is now built** (`carry.cpp`, 2026-07-11): the fifth playable slice, and the last unbuilt piece of the front half of the chain — the walk between the hole and the breaking stone. It stands on **no sim header** of its own; the furnace had `smelt.h`'s tabulated fires to honor, but the carry's only physics is geometry, and the one number it must not invent is the **distance** — so it does not. The walk is measured off `geology.h`'s real `DEPOSITS`: the breaking stone at an authored 80 m offset (#28, like the deposit centers), and the *far* tin ground at the true **323 m** (`sqrt(300²+120²)`, straight out of the field). The gesture is the **trip**, the carry's answer to the pan's swirl and the furnace's bellows: load what you can, walk it over, haul back, and again — a terminal can no more see a held walk than a held swirl, so a trip is an impulse, the road played out so you feel it. The rungs are **hands → a shouldered sack → a cart you stop and build**, and they only change how much moves per trip. The face is the **two piles and the road between them**, never a projected trip count. It teaches, without stating, the finding a corrected `geology.h` now carries: the pick's spoil is a **size distribution**, and liberation rides on it — the fine end comes up free (pannable at the hole), the coarse end locked (breaker only), and *how much frees at the face is the mineralogy's*. So the slice has **two** gestures: **wash** (screen the fines, pan them where you stand) and **trip** (haul the locked coarse, rungs hands → sack → cart). At this hole — the hard-rock copper hill — the wash wins only the oxide's thin free tail (~75 g of 3.6 kg copper), and nearly all the copper is locked in the coarse you must haul and crush; the report names the tin ground, where placer cassiterite comes up coarse and free and the same wash would take almost all of it, no stone touched (why placer tin was panned by one hand, hard rock built stamp mills). The locked coarse is still bigger than your hands, so the cart rungs still bite: **6 hand-trips / ~15 min** or **one cart trip** (its 3-min build shows on the clock and still wins), stopping early leaves *locked ore*, and the far tin ground (323 m vs the stone's 80 m) is only worth working once the cart exists. *(This paragraph said, until 2026-07-11, "nothing is liberated at the hole, the heap is one uniform grade, a shovel without a cart buries you." Monitored play refuted it — a pick, not a shovel, breaks rock across sizes and placer fines come up free.)* **Nothing of the front half is unbuilt now** — dig → carry → break → pan → furnace all run as playable slices, seven translation units under the strict flags, and the sim core produces exactly what each consumes.
